# Energetic and data analysis

> **Section numbering note.** The methodology is documented across three files. Sections 1--3 appear in [system-preparation](../system-preparation/system-preparation.md); sections 4--5 appear in [modeling-and-simulation](../modeling-and-simulation/modeling-and-simulation.md); sections 6--7 appear here.

## Scope

This section describes the pipeline applied to the raw configuration pools generated by stochastic rigid-body docking (SRD) and molecular dynamics (MD) sampling, as detailed in the [modeling-and-simulation](../modeling-and-simulation/modeling-and-simulation.md) section. The input to this pipeline is the complete set of **62,800 host-guest configurations per host-guest pair** (60,000 from SRD, 2,800 from MD). The output is a single **minimum binding energy** value per host-guest pair, derived from evaluating the SPE of the entire configuration pool, followed by filtration to pass candidates to geometry optimization, and a final SPE evaluation on the optimized complex structures, used as the primary metric for all subsequent selectivity analysis.

The pipeline consists of two sequential stages:

1. **Minimum binding energy calculation**: applying energy and steric filters to the raw configuration pool to identify a shortlist of physically meaningful low-energy candidates from each sampling method, followed by geometry optimization of those candidates and a final single-point energy evaluation to obtain the definitive reported value.
2. **Correlation and selectivity assessments**: using the reported binding energies (up to 148 host-guest pairs from 4 hosts × 37 guests; 136 converged in practice) to evaluate topology-driven selectivity trends within the pharmaceutical and cannabinoid guest sets.

## 6. Minimum binding energy calculation

The entire pool of host-guest complex structures was subjected to single-point energy (SPE) calculations following the template for the [AMS run file](../system-preparation/system-preparation.md/#single-point-run-ams):

```bash
#!/bin/sh

"$AMSBIN/ams" << eor

Task SinglePoint
System
    Atoms
        X   x y z
    End
    Lattice
        ax ay az
        bx by bz
        cx cy cz
    End
End

Engine DFTB
   Model GFN1-xTB
   DispersionCorrection D3-BJ
EndEngine

eor
```

The output is processed using the [mof-guest-toolkit](https://github.com/adricu12/mof-guest-toolkit) to extract the total energy for BE calculation using the [binding energy equation](../modeling-and-simulation/modeling-and-simulation.md/#binding-energy-equation) defined in the previous section:

$$BE = E_{\text{complex}} - (E_{\text{host}} + E_{\text{guest}})$$

The complete per-configuration energies (≈62,800 values per host-guest pair) constitute the bulk dataset and are **not committed** owing to their size. The screening and parsing logic is documented in the [energetic-data-analysis](../scripts/energetic-data-analysis.ipynb) Jupyter notebook and can be run against locally generated SPE outputs.

### 6.1. SPE-based candidate selection

#### Steric clash filter

Raw configurations from both SRD and MD contain a fraction of geometrically unphysical poses that must be excluded before any meaningful energetic ranking can be performed. Although the `host_guest` package constructs the convex hull of the accessible pore volume and places the guest center of mass within this region, rotating the guest until no hard-sphere overlap with framework atoms is detected, a subset of configurations nonetheless produces strongly repulsive SPE values. These arise from geometries in which the guest is placed at or very near the boundary of an interatomic contact: at such distances, the GFN1-xTB DFTB Hamiltonian, which uses atom-pair-specific Slater-Koster integrals with repulsive pair potentials calibrated at equilibrium geometries, interprets the short interatomic distance as a partial bonding interaction rather than a purely repulsive van der Waals contact. The result is an anomalously large positive or erratic electronic energy that reflects a failure of the semiempirical parameterization at compressed geometries rather than a physical host-guest interaction. Configurations with a raw SPE-derived BE exceeding **+10 kcal/mol** are therefore excluded as artifacts of near-boundary placement.

This threshold was established empirically by inspecting the distribution of raw interaction energies across the configuration pool. The BE distribution is bimodal: a dense population of physically meaningful configurations occupies the negative to moderately positive energy range, while a sparse high-energy tail corresponds to geometries with the near-contact artifacts described above. A cutoff of +10 kcal/mol captures the transition between these two populations. Configurations with a BE not exceeding +10 kcal/mol are retained for further analysis.

#### Top-candidate selection

After applying the steric clash filter, candidates are ranked by their raw GFN1-xTB SPE-derived BE. The **20 lowest-energy configurations** are selected from each sampling method independently:

- **SRD top-20**: selected from the combined pool of up to 60,000 filtered configurations across all three seeds.
- **MD top-20**: selected from the combined pool of up to 2,800 filtered snapshots across all seven trajectories (three at 300 K and four at elevated temperatures of 600 K, 900 K, 1,200 K, and 1,500 K).

This yields at most 40 candidate structures per host-guest pair (20 from SRD and 20 from MD) that proceed to geometry optimization.

The selection is performed per sampling method rather than from a merged pool to ensure that both methods contribute candidates regardless of any systematic energy offset between the two sets. Because SRD and MD explore different regions of configurational space and are guided by different energy functions, the raw SPE values from the two methods are not directly comparable without first relaxing the geometries. Treating them as separate candidate pools avoids discarding low-energy MD configurations that might rank poorly in the raw SRD pool due to force-field geometry artifacts, and vice versa.

The full pipeline implementation is in the [energetic-data-analysis](../scripts/energetic-data-analysis.ipynb) Jupyter notebook.

### 6.2. Geometry optimization of selected candidates

Each of the 40 candidate configurations is subjected to full geometry optimization at the GFN1-xTB level with D3(BJ) dispersion correction, as implemented in the AMS DFTB engine. Both host and guest atomic positions are relaxed simultaneously during this step; no constraints are imposed on the host framework. This represents a departure from the rigid-host approximation used during sampling and allows the framework to accommodate the guest geometry, capturing short-range structural relaxation effects that are inaccessible to the fixed-host sampling models. Lattice parameters are held fixed during complex optimization to avoid unphysical cell distortions at the GFN1-xTB level.

The representative [AMS run file](../system-preparation/system-preparation.md/#geometry-optimization-run-ams) for host-guest complex optimization is shown below (atom coordinates and lattice vectors shown as placeholders):

```bash
#!/bin/sh

"$AMSBIN/ams" << eor

Task GeometryOptimization

System
    Atoms
        X   x y z
        ...
    End
    Lattice
        ax ay az
        bx by bz
        cx cy cz
    End
End

Engine DFTB
   Model GFN1-xTB
   DispersionCorrection D3-BJ
EndEngine

eor
```

The AMS geometry optimization task applies the following default convergence criteria:

```
GeometryOptimization
    Convergence
        Energy (change): 1e-05 Ha
        (Cartesian) gradient: 0.001 Ha/Å
        (Cartesian) step size: 0.01 Å
        StressEnergyPerAtom : 0.0005 Ha    # When optimizing the lattice vectors
    End
End
```

### 6.3. Final single-point energy evaluation and reported minimum

Following geometry optimization, a GFN1-xTB single-point energy calculation is performed on each relaxed host-guest complex and on the corresponding isolated host and guest structures extracted from the optimized complex geometry using the [mof-guest-toolkit](https://github.com/adricu12/mof-guest-toolkit). The binding energy is then evaluated as:

$$BE_{\text{final}} = E_{\text{complex}}^{\text{opt}} - \left(E_{\text{host}}^{\text{opt}} + E_{\text{guest}}^{\text{opt}}\right)$$

where all three terms are evaluated at the GFN1-xTB level on the geometry obtained from the complex optimization. This ensures that the BE reflects the interaction energy at the relaxed geometry rather than at the raw sampled pose, and maintains consistency with the energy reference established during system preparation. The relaxed-candidate energies are an intermediate dataset and are not committed; their assembly is documented in the [energetic-data-analysis](../scripts/energetic-data-analysis.ipynb) Jupyter notebook.

For each host-guest pair, the **reported binding energy** is the minimum BE across all relaxed candidates from both sampling methods:

$$BE_{\text{reported}} = \min\left(\{BE_{\text{SRD},i}\}_{i=1}^{20},\ \{BE_{\text{MD},j}\}_{j=1}^{20}\right)$$

This value represents the most favorable relaxed host-guest interaction geometry identified by the combined sampling and optimization procedure at the GFN1-xTB level. The reported minima (136 of the 148 nominal pairs converged) are committed as `minimum_binding_energies.csv` in [`thesis/070_CORRELATION/`](../thesis/070_CORRELATION/) and are read in the [selectivity-analysis](../scripts/selectivity-analysis.ipynb) Jupyter notebook.

### 6.4. Complementary statistics for method assessment

Several diagnostics are computed on the raw configuration pools to assess the reliability of each sampling method, reported in full in [`method-assessments.md`](../results/method-assessments.md):

- **Steric-clash noise level** — the fraction of configurations excluded by the +10 kcal/mol threshold,

$$\%NL = \frac{\text{configurations excluded by threshold}}{N} \times 100$$

  where $N$ is the total number of configurations in the pool for a given host-guest pair and sampling method.
- **Sampling-volume / wall-contact fractions** — over the $N$ SRD configurations of a guest, with $d_i$ the closest guest-atom–framework contact in configuration $i$, the fraction reaching the wall-contact regime is

$$\%_{\le r} = \frac{\lvert\{\,i : d_i \le r\,\}\rvert}{N} \times 100, \qquad r \in \{5, 7\}\ \text{Å},$$

  reported with the mean and median of $\{d_i\}$ to diagnose the open-pore (csq) sampling bias (a low $\%_{\le 7\,\text{Å}}$ means the guest rarely reached the wall, so its minimum is a sampling floor).
- **Trajectory convergence** — for an MD minimum-BE series $\{B_t\}_{t=1}^{n}$, the first-half versus second-half drift

$$\mathrm{diff} = \frac{2}{n}\sum_{t \le n/2} B_t \;-\; \frac{2}{n}\sum_{t > n/2} B_t,$$

  used to flag host/temperature combinations ($\lvert\mathrm{diff}\rvert$ large relative to the BE scale) that have not finished exploring configuration space.
- **Fragment-misassignment outliers** — anomalously large binding energies (2–4× the typical magnitude, i.e. beyond the physical bulk range of ≈34–97 kcal/mol) flagged as artifacts of the force-field→DFTB handoff (proton transfer between host and guest fragments).

These statistics are computed on the (uncommitted) bulk configuration-pool data; refer to the [energetic-data-analysis](../scripts/energetic-data-analysis.ipynb) Jupyter notebook for the calculations and to [`method-assessments.md`](../results/method-assessments.md) for the results.

## 7. Selectivity analysis

### 7.1. Correlation with structural descriptors

Two correlation regimes are available, with very different statistical power, and only the first is reported as a formal correlation:

- **Within a host, across guests (N = 20 pharmaceuticals or ~15 cannabinoids):** for each framework, the reported minimum BEs are correlated (Pearson and Spearman) against guest descriptors. This is the regime used for the per-host structure–activity analysis in the results.
- **Across hosts, for a fixed property (N = 4 frameworks):** correlating host pore metrics (PLD, LCD, ASA, AV, POV) directly against binding energy uses only four points and is statistically unreliable; it is **not** reported as a formal correlation. Host comparisons rest instead on rank distributions and on the within-host correlations above.

The within-host correlation uses the descriptors established in the [structural characterization](../modeling-and-simulation/modeling-and-simulation.md/#4-structural-characterization) section.

The **Pearson correlation coefficient** between variables $X$ and $Y$ over $N$ host-guest pairs is:

$$r = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(X_i - \bar{X})^2}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2}}$$

The **Spearman rank correlation coefficient** is computed as the Pearson correlation of the rank-transformed variables:

$$\rho = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(R(X_i) - \overline{R_X})(R(Y_i) - \overline{R_Y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(R(X_i) - \overline{R_X})^2}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(R(Y_i) - \overline{R_Y})^2}}$$

where $R(X_i)$ denotes the rank of $X_i$ within the dataset. Spearman correlation is included alongside Pearson because the relationship between structural descriptors and binding energy is not assumed to be linear; monotonic but non-linear associations are captured by the rank-based measure. The guest descriptor set spans the major noncovalent-interaction classes: hydrogen bonding (H-bond donor/acceptor counts), lipophilicity (MolLogP), aromaticity (aromatic-ring count), conformational flexibility (rotatable bonds), electrostatics (maximum absolute partial charge), and 3D size/shape (radius of gyration, normalized principal-moment-of-inertia ratios, and minimum rotational width). Because many host–descriptor combinations are tested (≈10 descriptors × 4 hosts × 2 measures), individual nominally significant cells (p < 0.05) do not survive a multiple-comparison correction and are interpreted as suggestive rather than established.

Group differences in binding energy — pharmaceutical vs cannabinoid within a host, and (within the cannabinoids) carboxylic-acid vs neutral guests — are assessed with the two-sided **Mann–Whitney U test**, a rank-based test that makes no normality assumption and is robust to the small, unequal group sizes. For two samples of sizes $n_1$ and $n_2$,

$$U_1 = R_1 - \frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2}, \qquad U = \min(U_1, U_2),$$

where $R_1$ is the sum of the ranks of group 1 in the pooled ranking and $U_2 = n_1 n_2 - U_1$; the reported $p$-value is two-sided. This is the test behind the acid-vs-neutral cannabinoid result (acids bind ≈17 kcal/mol more strongly, $p \approx 0.005$).

### 7.2. Pharmaceutical selectivity assessment

Within the pharmaceutical guest set, the analysis is framed around two complementary perspectives. First, for each guest molecule, the four topologies are ranked by their reported minimum BE from most to least favorable (rank 1 = most negative BE). This per-guest ranking captures topology preference at the individual molecule level. Second, the total count of rank-1 placements per topology across all 20 pharmaceutical guests is evaluated as an aggregate measure of which framework most frequently provides the strongest interaction. In the context of pharmaceutical water remediation, where target contaminants are typically present at trace concentrations (ng/L to μg/L), the total rank-1 count provides a practically motivated metric: it identifies the topology that covers the broadest range of contaminants with the highest interaction strength, complementing the per-guest analysis.

### 7.3. Cannabinoid selectivity assessment

Within the cannabinoid guest set, cannabidiol (CBD) is used as the primary reference compound, reflecting its status as the most extensively studied non-psychoactive cannabinoid and its practical relevance as a target for selective separation. For each topology and each non-CBD cannabinoid guest, a relative binding energy difference is defined as:

$$\Delta BE_{\text{rel}} = BE_{\text{guest}} - BE_{\text{CBD}}$$

where both BEs refer to the same host topology. A negative $\Delta BE_{\text{rel}}$ indicates that the guest binds more favorably than CBD in that topology; a positive value indicates weaker binding relative to CBD. This metric reframes the analysis from absolute interaction strengths to topology-modulated discrimination between CBD and structurally related cannabinoids, which is the relevant quantity for assessing whether a given framework can preferentially capture or release CBD relative to its structural analogs and isomers.

The full data analysis pipeline is documented in the [energetic-data-analysis](./../scripts/energetic-data-analysis.ipynb) Jupyter notebook.
